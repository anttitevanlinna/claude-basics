# Enterprise AI Adoption Frameworks (Consulting Firms)

> CAVEAT: Based on knowledge through early 2025. Web search was unavailable. Consulting frameworks evolve and rebrand frequently — verify current naming and details.

---

## McKinsey

### Key Frameworks
- **"Rewired" framework (2023-2025):** McKinsey's signature digital/AI transformation model. Six enterprise capabilities: strategic roadmap, talent, operating model, technology, data, adoption/scaling. AI training sits under "talent" and "adoption."
- **AI adoption S-curve:** McKinsey distinguishes three phases — experiment (10% of workforce), scale (30-50%), and transform (org-wide). Training needs differ radically at each phase.
- **"Lighthouse" approach:** Start with 2-3 high-value use cases, prove ROI, then expand. Each lighthouse gets dedicated training and change management.

### What They Do Well
1. **Phase-appropriate training.** McKinsey argues (correctly) that what you teach in the experiment phase is different from the scale phase. Our framework is implicitly an experiment-phase tool — training the first 14 people. We should name this explicitly and sketch what "scale-phase training" looks like.
2. **ROI measurement integration.** Every McKinsey AI program ties training to measurable business outcomes. Our framework produces qualitative outputs (systems, recommendations) but no quantitative measurement. An IT director who needs to justify the training budget to the CFO needs numbers.
3. **Change management as first-class concern.** McKinsey treats resistance, fear, and organisational politics as engineering problems, not afterthoughts. Our Module 3 touches this (crux identification) but could be more explicit about the human/political dimension.

### What They Miss
1. **Over-abstraction.** McKinsey frameworks are beautiful PowerPoint but thin on the "what do we actually DO in the room on Tuesday?" question. Our framework is radically concrete — 45-minute modules, specific exercises, specific outputs. The gap between strategy and execution is exactly where we live.
2. **Tool-agnostic to a fault.** McKinsey frameworks work for "AI" generically. Our framework is Claude-specific, which means every exercise produces real output in the real tool. Generic AI training produces generic AI awareness.
3. **No agentic dimension.** McKinsey still largely treats AI as "a tool humans use" not "a participant in the workflow." Our training-where-Claude-is-co-participant is ahead of their frameworks.

## BCG

### Key Frameworks
- **"Build, embed, scale" model:** Three phases of AI capability development. Build = prove the technology works. Embed = integrate into workflows. Scale = roll out across the org.
- **BCG X (formerly BCG Gamma):** Their AI implementation arm. Offers "AI fluency" programs for executives and practitioners.
- **"10-20-70" rule:** 10% of AI transformation value comes from algorithms, 20% from technology, 70% from people and process changes. Training is part of the 70%.
- **"Responsible AI" toolkit:** Structured approach to AI governance — bias auditing, explainability, human oversight. Published frameworks for boards and C-suites.

### What They Do Well
1. **The 70% framing.** BCG's insistence that 70% of AI value comes from people/process change is a powerful sales argument for training programs. Our framework should cite this (or similar) when justifying investment to buyers. "You're spending on the technology. This training is how you capture the 70% that makes it actually work."
2. **Executive-specific programs.** BCG runs separate AI fluency sessions for C-suite that focus on strategic decisions, not tool usage. Our framework targets the IT team but the buyer (IT Director) has different needs than the participants. A 30-minute "executive briefing" add-on could be valuable.
3. **Responsible AI integration.** BCG weaves governance into capability building, not as a separate module. Similar philosophy to our Module 2, but BCG does it more systematically with assessment tools and maturity models.

### What They Miss
1. **Same over-abstraction problem as McKinsey.** Frameworks, not curricula. Principles, not exercises. We're concrete; they're strategic.
2. **No tool-specific training.** BCG's programs teach "AI thinking" not "Claude usage." For the IT team that needs to govern Claude specifically, generic AI fluency is necessary but insufficient.
3. **Cost structure.** BCG programs are priced for large enterprises. Our framework can be delivered in a half-day by one facilitator for 14 people. The economics are completely different.

## Deloitte

### Key Frameworks
- **"AI Dossier" / industry-specific AI adoption guides:** Deloitte publishes heavily on AI adoption by industry (financial services, healthcare, public sector). More practical than McKinsey/BCG.
- **"Trustworthy AI" framework:** Six dimensions — fair, robust, transparent, accountable, privacy-preserving, safe. Used for AI governance assessments.
- **Deloitte AI Academy:** Internal training program they've commercialised. Multi-tier: AI awareness (all employees), AI practitioner (builders), AI leader (executives).
- **Change management integration:** Deloitte's AI adoption work is often led by their Human Capital practice, not their tech practice. Training is embedded in broader organisational change programs.

### What They Do Well
1. **Tiered training model.** AI Awareness / AI Practitioner / AI Leader is a clean segmentation. Our framework maps to "AI Practitioner" with governance elements of "AI Leader." Naming our training's tier explicitly helps buyers understand where it fits in their broader AI upskilling plan.
2. **Trustworthy AI as governance framework.** Deloitte's six dimensions (fair, robust, transparent, accountable, privacy-preserving, safe) provide a structured vocabulary for the conversations we facilitate in Module 2. We could reference this as a recognised framework, giving our governance exercises more institutional credibility.
3. **Industry-specific adaptation.** Deloitte creates AI adoption guides per industry. Our framework has "plug points" for client customisation, but we haven't articulated industry-specific variations. For a training framework aimed at IT teams, industry context shapes the compliance requirements, data sensitivity, and use case priorities significantly.

### What They Miss
1. **Training =/= transformation.** Deloitte's programs are embedded in multi-month transformation engagements. Our framework is a standalone half-day that produces immediate, tangible outputs. For organisations not ready for (or able to afford) a full transformation program, our approach is more accessible.
2. **No experiential pedagogy.** Deloitte AI Academy is classroom + e-learning. No "build a system live, in the tool, right now." The gap between knowing about AI and knowing how to use AI is exactly where experiential learning wins.

## Cross-Cutting Implications for Our Framework

### Strengthen
1. **Name our phase explicitly.** We are an "experiment-to-scale bridge" — training the first cohort who will champion broader adoption. Say this. It helps buyers position the training within larger frameworks they may already be using.
2. **Add measurement.** Every consulting framework demands ROI metrics. Our post-work follow-up (Claude-led) could include a simple "before/after" assessment: time saved, tasks automated, confidence level. Even crude measurement beats no measurement.
3. **Provide a governance vocabulary.** Module 2 teaches governance by doing. Adding a reference to established frameworks (Deloitte's Trustworthy AI, BCG's Responsible AI) gives participants language they can use when writing policies and presenting to leadership.
4. **Executive briefing option.** A 30-minute pre-session briefing for the buyer/IT Director that frames the training in strategic terms (McKinsey's phases, BCG's 70% rule) would help secure buy-in and justify investment.

### Protect
1. **Concreteness.** Every consulting framework is abstract by design — they sell engagements to make it concrete. Our framework IS the concrete thing. Don't dilute it with framework-speak.
2. **Half-day, one facilitator, 14 people.** The economics of our model are radically different from a consulting engagement. This is a feature, not a limitation.
3. **Tool-specific, not tool-agnostic.** Generic AI training produces generic AI awareness. Claude-specific training produces Claude-capable teams. The specificity is the value.
